Overview

The pipeline

One number per team is the end of a chain: raw match history becomes strength signals, several match models turn those into outcome probabilities, a meta-learner blends them, and a tournament simulation turns match probabilities into a title chance.

The match-models layer is itself three models — a Bayesian Poisson goal model, a gradient-boosting classifier, and de-vigged market odds — stacked by the meta-learner.

The tournament

Monte Carlo — turning match models into a champion

The crux is a posterior-propagated, correlated simulation. For each simulation s = 1, …, N (N = 20,000), draw one posterior sample of every team's strengths θ(s) = {α(s), β(s), …}, then play the entire 48-team tournament under that single draw (group stage with FIFA tiebreakers → 8 best third-placed → knockout with extra time and penalties), sampling scorelines from the Poisson rates:

Because one θ(s) governs all of a team's matches in simulation s, outcomes are correlated — a secretly-strong team is strong in every match that sim — capturing tail behaviour that naive per-match sampling misses. The title probability is the Monte Carlo average:

Honest limits

Two approximations are documented honestly:

  • Group tiebreakers beyond goal difference and goals-for use a random draw.
  • The 8 best third-placed teams fill the round-of-32 slots by a fixed rule, not FIFA's exact combination table.

The match model trains on history; the 48-team bracket is rule-encoded — there is no precedent for it.
