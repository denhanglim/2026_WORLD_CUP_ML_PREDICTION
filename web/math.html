The pipeline

One number per team is the end of a chain: raw match history becomes strength signals, several match models turn those into outcome probabilities, a meta-learner blends them, and a tournament simulation turns match probabilities into a title chance.

  • Data →

The match-models layer is itself three models — a Bayesian Poisson goal model, a gradient-boosting classifier, and de-vigged market odds — stacked by the meta-learner.

2

Elo ratings

The baseline strength signal. Expected score for the home team, with home advantage H (zero on neutral ground):

Expected score (home) $$E_{\text{home}} = \frac{1}{1 + 10^{-\big((R_{\text{home}} + H) - R_{\text{away}}\big)/400}}$$

Update after a match (W ∈ {1, ½, 0} for win / draw / loss, zero-sum):

Rating update $$R' = R + K \cdot G \cdot (W - E)$$

Margin-of-victory multiplier G (goal difference d):

Margin multiplier $$G = \begin{cases} 1 & |d| \le 1 \\ 1.5 & |d| = 2 \\ \dfrac{11 + |d|}{8} & |d| \ge 3 \end{cases}$$

On the gain factor. K scales with match importance — World Cup = 60, continental = 50, qualifiers = 40, friendlies = 20. Computed from results alone — fully reproducible.

3

M1 — dynamic hierarchical Poisson goal model

The flagship.

Each team i has a latent attack αi,t and defense βi,t at period t. Expected goals:

$$\log \lambda_{\text{home}} = \mu + h\,\mathbb{1}[\text{not neutral}] + \alpha_{\text{home},t} - \beta_{\text{away},t}$$
$$\log \lambda_{\text{away}} = \mu + \alpha_{\text{away},t} - \beta_{\text{home},t}$$

Dynamic state-space

Strengths evolve as a Gaussian random walk across yearly periods:

$$\alpha_{i,t} = \alpha_{i,t-1} + \varepsilon_{i,t}, \qquad \varepsilon_{i,t} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_{\text{evo}}^2)$$

Hierarchical priors

Partial pooling shrinks small-sample teams toward the mean:

$$\alpha_{i,0},\,\beta_{i,0} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2), \quad \sigma_{\text{att}},\sigma_{\text{def}} \sim \text{HalfNormal}(1), \quad \sigma_{\text{evo}} \sim \text{HalfNormal}(0.15)$$
$$\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1), \quad h \sim \mathcal{N}(0.25, 0.5), \quad \rho \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.1)$$

Likelihood

Independent Poisson for the two goal counts:

$$\text{home goals} \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_{\text{home}}), \qquad \text{away goals} \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_{\text{away}})$$

Dixon–Coles low-score correction

The correction τ fixes the false independence on 0–0 / 1–0 / 0–1 / 1–1 scorelines, applied to the joint pmf:

$$\tau(x,y) = \begin{cases} 1 - \lambda_{\text{home}}\lambda_{\text{away}}\rho & (x,y)=(0,0) \\ 1 + \lambda_{\text{home}}\rho & (x,y)=(0,1) \\ 1 + \lambda_{\text{away}}\rho & (x,y)=(1,0) \\ 1 - \rho & (x,y)=(1,1) \\ 1 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Full joint scoreline probability:

$$P(X=x, Y=y) = \tau(x,y)\cdot \frac{\lambda_{\text{home}}^{x} e^{-\lambda_{\text{home}}}}{x!}\cdot \frac{\lambda_{\text{away}}^{y} e^{-\lambda_{\text{away}}}}{y!}$$

The posterior over all {α, β, μ, h, ρ, σ} is sampled with NUTS (Hamiltonian Monte Carlo). Match outcome probabilities come from summing the (normalised) score grid:

$$P(\text{home win}) = \sum_{x>y} P(x,y),\quad P(\text{draw}) = \sum_{x=y} P(x,y),\quad P(\text{away win}) = \sum_{x<y} P(x,y)$$

M2 & M3 — gradient boosting and the market

M2 — gradient boosting

A gradient-boosted classifier on engineered features (Elo difference, form, rest, rolling goals…) producing calibrated P(W / D / L). No heavy formula needed.

M3 — de-vigging the odds

Strip the bookmaker margin from decimal odds oi:

$$p_i = \frac{1/o_i}{\sum_{j} 1/o_j}$$
5

The meta-learner (stacking)

Stack the base models' probability vectors and combine with multinomial logistic regression — a softmax over a linear map of the concatenated base probabilities:

$$\mathbf{x} = \big[\,\mathbf{p}^{(\text{M1})} \,\|\, \mathbf{p}^{(\text{M2})} \,\|\, \mathbf{p}^{(\text{odds})}\,\big], \qquad P(\text{class}=k) = \frac{e^{\mathbf{w}_k^\top \mathbf{x} + b_k}}{\sum_{c} e^{\mathbf{w}_c^\top \mathbf{x} + b_c}}$$

Trained walk-forward on out-of-sample base predictions — no leakage.

6

How we score it (proper scoring rules)

Outcomes are ordinal (H ≺ D ≺ A). The Ranked Probability Score is primary:

RPS (primary) $$\text{RPS} = \frac{1}{r-1}\sum_{i=1}^{r-1}\left(\sum_{j=1}^{i} p_j - \sum_{j=1}^{i} o_j\right)^2, \quad r = 3$$

Also Brier ∑k (pk − ok)² and log-loss −log ptrue. Calibration is measured via Expected Calibration Error over B bins:

ECE (calibration) $$\text{ECE} = \sum_{b=1}^{B} \frac{n_b}{N}\,\big|\,\text{acc}_b - \text{conf}_b\,\big|$$

Backtest headline. The meta-learner reached RPS ≈ 0.1999 on WC 2010–2022, beating the Elo baseline ≈ 0.2022.

7

Monte Carlo — turning match models into a champion

The crux is a posterior-propagated, correlated simulation. For each simulation s = 1, …, N (N = 20,000), draw one posterior sample of every team's strengths θ(s) = {α(s), β(s), …}, then play the entire 48-team tournament under that single draw (group stage with FIFA tiebreakers → 8 best third-placed → knockout with extra time and penalties), sampling scorelines from the Poisson rates:

$$\theta^{(s)} \sim \text{posterior}, \qquad \text{champion}_s = \text{Simulate}\big(\text{bracket} \mid \theta^{(s)}\big)$$

Because one θ(s) governs all of a team's matches in simulation s, outcomes are correlated — a secretly-strong team is strong in every match that sim — capturing tail behaviour that naive per-match sampling misses. The title probability is the Monte Carlo average:

Title probability $$P(\text{team wins}) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{s=1}^{N}\mathbb{1}\big[\text{champion}_s = \text{team}\big]$$
!

Honest limits

Single-elimination is high variance — even the favourite is only about 1-in-5. The model is calibrated and uncertainty-aware, not an oracle.

Two approximations are documented honestly:

  • Group tiebreakers beyond goal difference and goals-for use a random draw.
  • The 8 best third-placed teams fill the round-of-32 slots by a fixed rule, not FIFA's exact combination table.

The match model trains on history; the 48-team bracket is rule-encoded — there is no precedent for it.
